<h2 id="market-order">Market order</h2>
<p>A market order is an order to buy/sell at the best available price.<br>
When you place a market order, you do not have any control over what price your market order will actually be filled at.</p>
<h2 id="limit-order">Limit order</h2>
<p>A limit order is an order placed to either buy below or sell above the market at a certain price.</p>
<ul>
<li>you place a “Buy Limit” order to buy at or below a specified price</li>
<li>you place a “Sell Limit” order to sell at a specified price or better</li>
</ul>
<p>You use this type of entry order when you believe the price will reverse upon hitting the price you specified.</p>
<h2 id="stop-entry-order">Stop entry order</h2>
<p>A stop entry order is an order placed to buy above the market or sell below the market at a certain price.</p>
<ul>
<li>you place a “Buy Stop” order to buy at a price above the market price, and it is triggered when the market price touches or goes through the “Buy Stop” price</li>
<li>you place a “Sell Stop” order to sell when a specified price is reached</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="stop-loss-order">Stop loss order</h2>
<p>Prevents additional losses if the price goes against you.</p>
<ul>
<li>if you are in a long position, it is a “Sell Stop” order</li>
<li>if you are in a short position, it is a “Buy Stop” order</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="trailing-stop">Trailing stop</h2>
<p>This order is attached to a trade that moves as the price fluctuates.</p>
<ul>
<li>e.g. we go short USD/JPY at 90.80 with a trailing stop of 20 pips
<ul>
<li>so originally our stop loss is at 91.00</li>
<li>the price goes down to 90.60 so the trailing stop would go down from 91.00 to 90.80 (or breakeven)</li>
<li>the trailing stop will stay at this level, it will not widen if the market goes higher against you</li>
<li>the price keeps going down to 90.40, so the trailing stop goes to 90.60 and locks in 20 pips profit</li>
<li>once the market hits the trailing stop price, the position will be closed</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="time-in-force-orders-tif">Time-in-force orders (TIF)</h2>
<p>These orders are special instructions that tell the broker how long an order should remain active before it is cancelled if not executed.</p>
<h3 id="good-for-the-day-gfd">1.) Good for the day (GFD)</h3>
<p>This is the default <strong>TIF</strong> order for most trading platforms.<br>
The order remains active until the end of the day.</p>
<h3 id="good-till-cancelled-gtc">2.) Good 'till cancelled (GTC)</h3>
<p>The order remains active until you decide to cancel it.<br>
The broker might have restrictions on how long this order can remain active (e.g. 90 days).</p>
<h3 id="immediate-or-cancel-ioc">3.) Immediate or cancel (IOC)</h3>
<p>The order must be filled immediately in full or in part. Any portion of the order that cannot be filled immediately is canceled.</p>
<h3 id="fill-or-kill-fok">4.) Fill or kill (FOK)</h3>
<p>Similar to <strong>IOC</strong> but stricter. The entire order must be filled immediately, otherwise the whole order is canceled.</p>
<h3 id="good-till-date-gtd">5.) Good 'till date (GTD)</h3>
<p>The order remains active until a specified  date. If it’s not filled until the date, it is canceled.</p>
<h2 id="conditional-orders">Conditional orders</h2>
<p>These orders become active based on specific conditions being met.</p>
<h3 id="one-cancels-the-other-oco">1.) One-cancels-the-other (OCO)</h3>
<p>Consists of two orders linked together, if one order is executed, the other one is canceled.</p>
<h3 id="one-triggers-the-other-oto">2.) One-triggers-the-other (OTO)</h3>
<p>Consists of two orders linked together, the execution of one order triggers the other one.</p>
