<h1 id="forex-history">Forex history</h1>
<h2 id="the-early-days-when-fx-trading-was-a-members-only-club">The early days, when FX trading was a members-only club</h2>
<p>FX trading emerged in the 1970s after the Bretton Woods system was dismantled in 1971.</p>
<p>The Bretton Woods Agreement was an international monetary system established in July 1944 at the United Nations Monetary and Financial Conference held in Bretton Woods, New Hampshire.<br>
The agreement aimed to create a stable global economic environment following the chaos caused by WWII and the Great Depression.<br>
Representatives from 44 countries attended the conference, with the primary architects being John Maynard Keynes from the U.K. and Harry Dexter White from the U.S.<br>
The agreement led to the creation of a fixed exchange rate system, where the currencies of participating countries were pegged to the U.S. dollar, which in turn was pegged to gold at a fixed rate of $35 per ounce. This system aimed to prevent competitive currency devaluations and promote international trade and investment.<br>
The agreement also established two significant international financial institutions:</p>
<p>1.) International Monetary Fund (IMF)<br>
2.) World Bank</p>
<p>The IMF was created to monitor exchange rates and provide financial assistance to countries facing balance of payment problems.<br>
The World Bank’s main purpose was to provide funding for post-war reconstruction and development projects in member countries.</p>
<p>The system began to break down in the 1960s.<br>
The main problem was that the U.S. was running a large trade deficit, so it was importing more goods/services than it was exporting. This caused the U.S. to lose gold, which was the basis of the system.<br>
In 1971, due to economic pressures and inflation, the U.S. government announced that it would no longer convert U.S. dollars into gold. This ended the Bretton Woods system and led to the collapse of the fixed exchange rate system and the emergence of a floating exchange rate system, which we still use today.</p>
<p>Because of this shift to a floating exchange rate system, the currency speculation increased and started the way to the birth of the modern foreign exchange market.<br>
Back then trading was only accessible to banks, hedge funds and large corporations. Retail participation was basically non-existent because:</p>
<ul>
<li>high capital requirements</li>
<li>limited access =&gt; only institutions with direct access to interbank dealing could trade currencies</li>
<li>manual processes =&gt; trading was conducted over the phone or via telex</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="the-1990s-enter-the-internet-and-a-new-era-of-retail-fx-trading">The 1990s: enter the internet and a new era of retail FX trading</h2>
<p>In the 1990s, because of the booming of the internet, the banks began creating their own trading platforms. These platforms were designed to stream live quotes to their clients so that they could instantly execute trades themselves.<br>
Some people also introduced internet-based trading platforms for individual traders.<br>
Known as “retail forex brokers”, these entities made it easy for individuals to trade by allowing smaller trade sizes. Early adapters of online trading had to deal with slow internet speeds, unreliable connections.<br>
As internet speeds increased, more and more online trading platforms popped up.</p>
<h2 id="the-2000s-the-explosion-of-online-forex-brokers-and-trading-platforms">The 2000s: the explosion of online forex brokers and trading platforms</h2>
<p>In the early 2000s, there was an explosion of online brokers. Many brokers provided demo accounts allowing traders to participate with virtual money.<br>
The increased competition among brokers led to tighter spreads, lower transaction costs, and an expansion of trading products.</p>
<p>Financial regulators stepped in to protect individual traders from shady forex brokers.<br>
Prominent regulatory agencies: US Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC), the UK Financial Conduct Authority (FCA), the Australian Securities and Investments Commission (ASIC), the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA).<br>
These regulators have implemented measures such as leverage restrictions, negative balance protection, and mandatory segregation of customer funds to protect retail traders.</p>
<h2 id="the-2010s-technological-advancements">The 2010s: technological advancements</h2>
<ul>
<li>mobile trading</li>
<li>algorithmic trading =&gt; advances in algorithmic trading and high-frequency trading technologies enabled retail traders to use automated strategies and sophisticated analysis tools</li>
<li>tighter regulations =&gt; regulators continued to implement stricter rules on forex brokers, including leverage limits, mandatory disclosures, KYC requirements, and eligibility criteria for new accounts</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="the-present-day-a-highly-accessible-market">The present day: a highly accessible market</h2>
<ul>
<li>advanced trading platforms</li>
<li>diverse market participants</li>
<li>global community</li>
</ul>
