<h1 id="forex-market-players">Forex market players</h1>
<p>Until the late 1990s, only the people who had millions could trade forex.<br>
Forex was originally intended to be used by bankers and large institutions. But, because of the rise of the internet, online forex brokers are now able to offer trading accounts to retail traders.</p>
<h2 id="central-banks">Central Banks</h2>
<p>Key central banks include: European Central Bank, the Bank of England, the Federal Reserve.<br>
Central banks are responsible for managing a country’s monetary policy and regulating the supply of money and interest rates. By doing this, they can affect currency valuation.<br>
There are times when central banks intervene directly or verbally in the forex market when they want to realign exchange rates.<br>
Sometimes central banks think that their currency is priced too high or too low, so they start massive sell/buy operations to alter the exchange rates.<br>
Central bank actions can have a big impact on currency values. For example, an interest rate hike by a central bank can strengthen its currency, while quantitative easing programs can lead to currency depreciation.</p>
<ul>
<li><mark><strong>Quantitative Easing</strong></mark> =&gt; (QE), is an unconventional monetary policy used by central banks to stimulate the economy when conventional monetary policy has stopped working.
<ul>
<li>it is referred to as “printing money”, but there are no actual bank notes are being printed</li>
<li>money is simply electronically created or “keystroked”</li>
<li>“keystroking money” or “typing up money” are more accurate phrases</li>
<li>so with this “new money”, central banks can buy assets from commercial banks and other private institutions</li>
<li>these assets are usually government bonds but depending on the central bank, it can also be mortgage-backed securities (MBS) and corporate bonds</li>
<li>e.g. the Fed buys Treasury Bonds from investors, like a U.S. pension fund</li>
<li><strong>How does the pension fund get paid?</strong>
<ul>
<li>the pension fund doesn’t get cash</li>
<li>instead, the Fed tells the bank (whichever bank the pension fund uses, e.g. Bank of America), to “add $100 million to the pension fund’s checking account”</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>What changes at the Bank of America?</strong>
<ul>
<li>liability side =&gt; the bank now owes the pension fund $100 million more (this is a new deposit)</li>
<li>asset side =&gt; the Fed gives the bank $100 million in reserves</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>So in one simple move:</strong>
<ul>
<li>the pension fund has more money in its bank account</li>
<li>the bank now has more deposits and more reserves</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>The two types of money that both go up at the same time:</strong>
<ul>
<li>reserves =&gt; “central bank money”, this is the money that only banks have, they use it to pay each other</li>
<li>deposits =&gt; “normal money”, people and companies use this money</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="super-banks">Super Banks</h2>
<p>Some of these banks include: Citi, JPMorgan, UBS, Barclays, Deutsche Bank, Goldman Sachs, HSBC, Bank of America.</p>
<p>Since the forex market is decentralized, the largest banks determine the exchange rates.<br>
They facilitate currency trading for their clients (corporations, governments, financial institutions). They are responsible for most of the daily trading volume, and they make their money by acting as market makers. This means they are constantly buying/selling currencies, pocketing the difference between bid/ask prices. They are collectively known as the interbank market or Interdealer market, also known as “flow monsters”.<br>
They often have access to superior information and advanced analytics which gives them an edge against smaller traders.<br>
Their trading activities can affect short-term currency price movements.</p>
<h2 id="electronic-liquidity-providers-elps">Electronic Liquidity Providers (ELPs)</h2>
<p>They are specialized firms that play a big role in the forex market.</p>
<p>They use advanced computer systems and trading algorithms to provide liquidity. This means they constantly show buy (bid) and sell (ask) prices for different currency pairs.</p>
<p>By doing this, they make sure there is always a buyer or seller available, so trades can happen quickly and smoothly.</p>
<p><strong>Some well-known ELPs:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Citadel Securities</li>
<li>Flow Traders</li>
<li>HC Tech</li>
<li>Jump Trading</li>
<li>Virtu Financial</li>
<li>XTX Markets</li>
</ul>
<p>They are also called <strong>Principal Trading Firms (PTFs)</strong>.</p>
<p><strong>How they differ from traditional banks:</strong> Unlike banks, ELPs mainly use <strong>high-frequency trading (HFT)</strong> and other fast computer-driven strategies. HFT allows them to make thousands of trades every second, profiting from very small price changes.</p>
<p>They rely on super-fast technology, such as co-located servers and low-latency networks, to trade at lightning speed.</p>
<p>In short, ELPs help the forex market run efficiently by making it easy for everyone to buy and sell currencies quickly at good prices.</p>
<h2 id="large-commercial-companies">Large Commercial Companies</h2>
<p>Big multinational companies (like Apple, Toyota, and ExxonMobil) regularly buy and sell currencies in the forex market.</p>
<p>They do this for three main reasons:</p>
<ul>
<li>To pay for goods and services in foreign countries</li>
<li>To protect (hedge) against currency price changes that could hurt their profits
<ul>
<li><mark><strong>Hedge</strong></mark> =&gt; protecting their money from currency risk</li>
<li>Imagine Apple needs to pay a Japanese supplier ¥10 billion in 3 months for parts</li>
<li>Today’s exchange rate: 1 USD = 150 JPY So Apple expects to pay about $66.7 million</li>
<li>But what if the yen gets stronger in 3 months? Now 1 USD = only 130 JPY → Apple would need to pay <strong>$76.9 million</strong> instead</li>
<li>That extra $10 million would eat into Apple’s profits</li>
<li><strong>Hedging</strong> solves this problem. Apple buys Japanese yen <strong>now</strong> (or locks in today’s rate) so that no matter how the exchange rate changes later, they still pay roughly $66.7 million. They remove the risk</li>
<li>In short: <strong>Hedging = protecting profits from bad currency moves</strong></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>To manage cash from international business =&gt; companies need to handle and move money that comes in different currencies
<ul>
<li>Toyota (Japan) sells cars in the USA and earns <strong>US dollars</strong>. But Toyota pays its workers, suppliers, and bills in <strong>Japanese yen</strong></li>
<li>So every month, Toyota receives dollars from America, but needs yen to run its business in Japan</li>
<li>They go to the forex market to:
<ul>
<li>Convert those US dollars into Japanese yen</li>
<li>Move money between countries smoothly</li>
<li>Make sure they always have the right currency at the right time</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>When they make large transactions, it can sometimes move exchange rates.</p>
<p>They usually trade through commercial banks (not directly in the interbank market).<br>
Big mergers and acquisitions between companies can also cause currency movements =&gt; when big companies from different countries merge or one buys the other (cross-border M&amp;A), it often causes currency exchange rates to move.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Why it happens:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>The buyer (acquirer) usually needs to pay a huge amount in the <strong>target company’s currency</strong></li>
<li>This creates sudden <strong>large demand</strong> for that currency in the forex market</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Easy example:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>A big American company (like Apple) buys a large Japanese company for $10 billion
<ul>
<li>Apple must convert <strong>US dollars into Japanese yen</strong> to pay for the deal.</li>
<li>They buy billions of yen all at once (or in big chunks).</li>
<li>This extra demand makes the <strong>yen stronger</strong> (it appreciates) against the US dollar.</li>
<li>At the same time, the <strong>US dollar may weaken</strong> slightly against the yen.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Even the announcement of the deal can move the currency before any money is actually exchanged, because traders expect the big currency purchase and react early.</p>
<p>Even though they don’t trade to make profit like banks or traders, their activity is still important for the forex market.</p>
<h2 id="the-speculators">The Speculators</h2>
<p>They actively seek to profit from currency price movements.<br>
Speculation in the forex market involves the buying and selling of currencies with the view of making profit. They focus on price fluctuations.<br>
It is called speculation because of the uncertainty involved since no one can know for sure whether a currency pair’s price will go up or down. Traders asses the likelihood of either scenario before placing a trade. Speculative trading adds liquidity to the market and can lead to increased volatility.</p>
<p><strong>Two kinds of speculators:</strong></p>
<p>1.) Hedge funds<br>
2.) Retail traders</p>
<h3 id="hedge-funds-and-prop-firms">1.) Hedge funds and prop firms</h3>
<p>(known as the “smart money” in forex)</p>
<p>These are professional traders who use smart, high-tech strategies to make profits in the forex market.</p>
<p>They analyze the market deeply, use computer algorithms, and take calculated risks. They often borrow money (use leverage) to make their trades much bigger.</p>
<p>Because they trade in huge volumes, their buying and selling can quickly move currency prices and create volatility (big price swings) in the short term.</p>
<h3 id="retail-traders">2.) Retail traders</h3>
<p>These are ordinary people who trade currencies from their homes using computers, phones, or tablets.</p>
<p>Thanks to easy online platforms and free learning resources, more and more individuals are joining the forex market. They range from beginners trading for fun to serious traders who want to do it full-time.</p>
<p>Even though each person trades small amounts, together they add liquidity to the market and can influence short-term price moves. Sometimes their overall mood (sentiment) can even drive bigger trends.</p>
<p>They may not move the market like the big players, but they are an important part of the forex world.</p>
