<h1 id="forex-trading-sessions">Forex trading sessions</h1>
<p>The forex market can be broken up into 4 major trading sessions: Sydney session, Tokyo session, London session, and New York session.<br>
The market has three peak trading sessions: Asian, European, and North American session.<br>
This is known as the “forex 3-session system”. Traders often focus on one of the 3 trading sessions.<br>
Each session has different level of volatility and liquidity.</p>
<ul>
<li><mark><strong>International Date Line</strong></mark> =&gt; (IDL), an imaginary line that runs from the North Pole to the South Pole and demarcates the change of one calendar day to the next.</li>
</ul>
<p>Trading starts in Wellington, New Zealand.<br>
The market is closed during the weekend and on Christmas and New Year’s Day.</p>

</table><h2 id="tokyo-session">Tokyo session</h2>
<ul>
<li>Japan is the third-largest forex trading center in the world (yen is the third most traded currency)</li>
<li>around 20% of all forex trading volume takes place during the Asian session</li>
<li>it’s not all coming from Tokyo, but from Hong Kong and Singapore too</li>
<li>the main market participants during the Tokyo session are commercial companies (exporters) and central banks (Japan’s economy is heavily export-dependent)</li>
<li>liquidity can sometimes be very thin</li>
<li>most of the action takes place early in the session when more economic data is released</li>
<li>moves in the Tokyo session could set the tone for the rest of the day</li>
<li>traders in later sessions will look at what happened during the Tokyo session to help organize and evaluate what strategies to take in other sessions</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="london-session">London session</h2>
<ul>
<li>a large chunk of forex transactions take place during this time</li>
<li>this leads to high liquidity and potentially lower transaction costs</li>
<li>most trends begin during this session and they typically continue until the beginning of the New York session</li>
<li>volatility tends to die down in the middle of the session, as traders go off to eat lunch before waiting for the New York trading period</li>
<li>trends can sometimes reverse at the end of the session, as European traders may decided to lock in profits</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="new-york-session">New York session</h2>
<ul>
<li>high liquidity during the morning (local time)</li>
<li>most economic reports are released near the start of the session</li>
<li>once European markets close, liquidity and volatility tend to die down during the afternoon session</li>
<li>there is very little movement on Friday afternoon</li>
<li>on Fridays, there is the chance of reversals in the second half of the session, as traders close their positions before the weekend</li>
</ul>
