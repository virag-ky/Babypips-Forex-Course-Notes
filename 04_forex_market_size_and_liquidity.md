<h1 id="forex-market-size-and-liquidity">Forex market size and liquidity</h1>
<ul>
<li>the majority of forex trading happens in the interbank market</li>
<li>the forex market is considered an over-the-counter (OTC) market, because the entire market runs electronically within a network of banks and non-bank financial institutions (NBFIs)</li>
<li>the U.S. dollar is the most traded, then the euro, then the yen</li>
<li>the U.S. dollar plays a central role in the forex market:
<ul>
<li>the U.S. economy is the largest economy in the world</li>
<li>the U.S. dollar is the reserve currency of the world =&gt; central banks and governments hold huge piles of dollars as their main emergency or backup money, so the whole world trusts and uses it for important international stuff</li>
<li>e.g. Saudi Arabia sells oil to Japan =&gt; they get paid in dollars</li>
<li>or Brasil wants to buy coffee machines from Germany =&gt; the deal is often priced and paid in dollars</li>
<li>countries can quickly buy things from anywhere without always swapping their own money first</li>
<li>this gives the U.S. some big advantages, like being able to borrow money cheaply from the rest of the world, but it also means many countries’ economies feel the effects when U.S. interest rates or policies change</li>
<li>the U.S. has a stable political system</li>
<li>the U.S. is the world’s sole military superpower =&gt; has the biggest power</li>
<li>the U.S. dollar is the medium of exchange for many cross-border transactions
<ul>
<li>e.g. the oil is priced in dollars, also called “petrodollars”</li>
<li>e.g. Japan wants to buy oil from Saudi Arabia, they can do that with dollars, if they don’t have, they need to sell yen first and buy U.S. dollars</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="the-main-functions-of-the-forex-market">The main functions of the forex market</h2>
<ol>
<li>to transfer funds from one currency to another</li>
<li>to provide short-term credit to finance trade between countries =&gt; it gives quick, temporary loans so businesses can buy/sell stuff internationally without paying everything upfront
<ul>
<li>e.g. Brazilian company orders coffee machines from Germany, they don’t have to pay immediately, they use a short-term forex credit to get the machines now and pay later when they sell the coffee =&gt; this helps trade flow smooth without waiting for cash</li>
<li>to make the deal safe and possible, the Brazilian company gets a short-term credit from their own bank or through the forex/trade finance system =&gt; this credit is in foreign currency, like in euros, because Germany wants euros</li>
<li>the German company gets paid in one of these ways:
<ul>
<li>through a letter of credit (most common for international deals) =&gt; the Brazilian bank promise guarantees payment to the German company as soon as the machines are shipped and documents are shown, the German company gets money quickly from the bank, not waiting for the Brazilian company to sell coffees</li>
<li>or the Brazilian company borrows money to pay the German company right away</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>then the Brazilian company repays later</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>to avoid or “hedge” (protection or defense against financial loss) against foreign exchange rate fluctuations from when a transaction is initiated and when payment is received</li>
<li>to speculate</li>
</ol>
<p>While commercial and financial transactions are part of the trading volume, most of the trading volume comes from traders that buy/sell based on the short-term price movements of currency pairs.</p>
